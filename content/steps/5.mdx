<SafetyCallout variant="warning" title="Subcutaneous Only — Unless Otherwise Specified">
  This guide covers subcutaneous (SubQ) injection, which is the standard route for most research peptides. Some peptides may be administered intramuscularly (IM) — if your protocol specifies IM, the technique differs. If you are unsure, default to SubQ.
</SafetyCallout>

## Subcutaneous vs. Intramuscular

**Subcutaneous (SubQ)**: Injection into the fatty tissue just below the skin. This is what most peptides use. Absorption is slower and more sustained than IV, and the technique is simple and low-risk.

**Intramuscular (IM)**: Injection into muscle tissue. Faster absorption, but requires different needle length (1–1.5") and technique. More risk of hitting nerves or blood vessels.

Unless your peptide protocol specifically calls for IM, use SubQ.

## Injection Site Selection

Subcutaneous injections are typically given in areas with enough fat tissue:

- **Abdomen** — 2 inches (5cm) away from the navel. Most common site. Easy to reach and see.
- **Outer thigh** — upper, outer quadrant
- **Upper outer arm** — requires assistance or flexibility
- **Flanks** — the sides of the torso

**Rotate injection sites** — do not inject in the exact same spot repeatedly. Repeated injections in one area can cause lipodystrophy (fatty tissue damage), scarring, and absorption issues. Keep a simple log of where you injected each day.

## Procedure

**1. Wash your hands.**
With soap and water for at least 20 seconds. This is the single most important step for preventing infection.

**2. Choose and expose your injection site.**
Sit or lie in a comfortable position. The skin should be clean and dry. Avoid broken skin, bruises, moles, or scars.

**3. Wipe the injection site with an alcohol swab.**
Use a single swipe outward from the center. Let it air dry completely — injecting through wet alcohol stings and can cause tissue irritation.

**4. Pinch the skin.**
Using your non-dominant hand, pinch a fold of skin and subcutaneous fat between your thumb and forefinger. This elevates the SubQ layer and reduces the chance of hitting muscle.

**5. Insert the needle.**
Hold the syringe like a pen or dart. Insert the needle at a **45° to 90° angle** depending on the amount of SubQ tissue:
- Thin individuals with less fat tissue → 45°
- Average to more fat tissue → 90°

Insert smoothly and quickly — hesitation makes it hurt more. The needle should go in without resistance.

**6. Release the skin pinch.**
Once the needle is in, gently release the skin fold. This ensures the medication goes into SubQ tissue, not a pinched fold.

**7. Inject slowly.**
Depress the plunger steadily over 5–10 seconds. Injecting too quickly can cause burning or a wheal (small raised area) under the skin.

**8. Remove the needle.**
Pull straight out at the same angle you inserted. Do not wiggle or rotate.

**9. Apply gentle pressure.**
Use a clean cotton ball or gauze to apply gentle pressure for 10–15 seconds. Do not rub — this can spread the solution and cause bruising.

**10. Dispose of the needle immediately.**
See Step 6.

<SafetyCallout variant="info" title="First-Timer Tips">
  The anticipation is almost always worse than the injection itself with a 29–31g insulin needle. These needles are very fine — you will barely feel the insertion. Breathe out as you insert to reduce tension. If you are very anxious, practice the motion with the cap on first.
</SafetyCallout>

## Common Reactions and What They Mean

### Normal

- Slight sting during or after injection
- Small red spot or pinprick mark at the site
- Minor bruising (especially in the abdomen)
- Small raised wheal that resolves in 10–20 minutes

### Monitor Closely

- Redness that spreads beyond 1–2cm from the injection site
- Warm or hot skin around the site
- Pain that increases over hours rather than fading
- Swelling that persists beyond 24 hours

### Seek Medical Attention

- Rapidly spreading redness (erythema) over a large area
- Pus, discharge, or abscess formation
- Fever after injection
- Systemic symptoms: chills, rigors, difficulty breathing

<SafetyCallout variant="danger" title="Injection Site Infection Is Serious">
  An abscess at an injection site is not just painful — it can become life-threatening if it progresses to cellulitis or sepsis. Do not "wait and see" if you see signs of spreading infection after 24 hours. Go to urgent care or an emergency room.
</SafetyCallout>

## Systemic Reactions to Watch For

Beyond the injection site, some peptides cause systemic effects with the first few doses:

- **Flushing, warmth, tingling** — common with GHRP/secretagogues, usually transient
- **Nausea** — common with GLP-1 agonists, usually dose-dependent
- **Headache or dizziness** — monitor and hydrate
- **Water retention** — common early with growth hormone secretagogues
- **Hypoglycemia** — rare but possible, especially if injecting near meal times with insulin-sensitizing peptides

Always inject your first dose of a new peptide at a time when you can rest and monitor yourself for at least 2 hours.

After injection, your syringe and needle need to be disposed of safely.
