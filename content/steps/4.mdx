## Overview

Drawing is the process of pulling a precise volume of reconstituted peptide from the vial into your insulin syringe. Accuracy here directly affects your dose.

You will do this before every injection — not in advance. Draw only what you need for a single dose each time.

## What You Need

- Reconstituted peptide vial (refrigerated)
- Insulin syringe (appropriate size for your draw volume — see Step 2)
- Alcohol swabs
- Your calculated syringe unit mark (from Step 3 calculator)

## Procedure

**1. Remove the vial from the refrigerator.**
Let it sit at room temperature for 5–10 minutes. Injecting cold liquid is uncomfortable and can cause local irritation.

**2. Wipe the vial rubber stopper with an alcohol swab.**
Let it air dry for 15–20 seconds.

**3. Pull air into your syringe equal to your draw volume.**
If you are drawing 10 units, pull 10 units of air into the syringe first. This creates positive pressure in the vial and makes drawing easier.

**4. Insert the needle into the vial and push the air in.**
Invert the vial so the needle points up (vial upside down). The needle tip should be submerged in the liquid.

**5. Draw back the plunger to your target unit mark.**
Pull slowly and steadily. If you overshoot, push a little back and re-draw. Do not worry about small air bubbles — they are not dangerous in subcutaneous injections, but you can flick the syringe and push them out if you prefer.

**6. Remove the needle from the vial.**
Pull straight out without bending the needle. Do not recap using two hands — if you need to recap, use the one-hand scoop method to avoid needlestick injury.

**7. Verify your dose.**
Hold the syringe up to a light source and confirm you are at the correct unit mark. It is easier to verify before than after.

<SafetyCallout variant="warning" title="Never Reuse Needles">
  Insulin needles are single-use. Reusing needles introduces contamination risk, causes microscopic barbing that increases injection pain, and can push skin cells or bacteria into your vial. Use a fresh needle for every injection.
</SafetyCallout>

## Handling Air Bubbles

Small air bubbles in an insulin syringe are normal and not dangerous for subcutaneous injection (this is different from intravenous administration, where air embolism is a risk). However, air displaces liquid, so large bubbles can affect your dose accuracy.

To remove bubbles:
1. Hold the syringe vertically with the needle pointing up
2. Flick the barrel a few times to encourage bubbles to rise
3. Gently depress the plunger until the bubbles are expelled
4. Re-draw to your target mark if needed

## Reading Your Syringe

Insulin syringes are marked in units, not milliliters. The number of units per ml depends on your syringe type:

- **U-100**: 100 units per ml → 10 units = 0.1ml
- **U-50**: 50 units per ml → 10 units = 0.2ml
- **U-40**: 40 units per ml → 10 units = 0.25ml

This is why the syringe type matters when using the calculator. A syringe filled to "10 units" contains very different volumes depending on the syringe type.

<SafetyCallout variant="info" title="Mark Your Target Before You Draw">
  Before inserting the needle into the vial, use a small piece of tape or your thumbnail to mark the target unit mark on the syringe barrel. This makes it easier to stop accurately while the vial is inverted.
</SafetyCallout>

With a loaded syringe, you are ready to inject.
