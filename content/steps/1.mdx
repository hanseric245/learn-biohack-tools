## Sourcing Research Peptides

Research peptides exist in a regulatory gray area in most countries. They are sold as "for research use only, not for human consumption" — but they are widely used by individuals who self-administer.

### What to Look for in a Vendor

A reputable vendor should provide:

- **Third-party Certificate of Analysis (CoA)** — mass spectrometry and HPLC purity testing from an independent lab, not just their own
- **Sterility testing** — especially important for pre-mixed solutions
- **Clear labeling** — peptide name, lot number, mass (mg), manufacturing date
- **Responsive customer service** — a sign they stand behind their product

### Red Flags to Avoid

- No CoA, or CoA provided only by their internal lab
- Unusually low prices (significantly below market rate)
- No lot number on vials
- Vague or missing ingredient information
- Anonymous websites with no contact information

### Community Resources for Vendor Vetting

The peptide community actively shares vendor reviews. Check:

- **r/Peptides** on Reddit — pinned vendor threads and community reports
- **Longecity forums** — longer-form discussion and vendor history
- **Peptide Sciences, Limitless Life Peptides, Amino Asylum** — commonly discussed vendors (do your own current due diligence; the landscape changes)

## Supplies You Will Need

Beyond the peptide itself, you need the following before you can proceed safely.

### Reconstitution Supplies

| Item | Purpose | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| Bacteriostatic water (BW) | Solvent for reconstitution | Medical supply or compounding pharmacy |
| Alcohol swabs | Sterilize vial tops and injection sites | Pharmacy |
| Reconstitution needles (18–21g, 1–1.5") | Draw BW into vial | Medical supply |
| Insulin syringes (U-100, 29–31g) | Draw and inject doses | Pharmacy (no Rx needed in most US states) |

### Storage Supplies

| Item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Refrigerator | Store reconstituted vials (2–8°C / 36–46°F) |
| Freezer | Long-term storage of unreconstituted powder |
| Sharps container | Safe needle disposal |

### Optional but Recommended

- **Gloves** — nitrile, for handling vials and needles
- **Gauze or cotton balls** — post-injection pressure
- **Label tape and marker** — date your vials after reconstitution
- **Small pill case or organizer** — keep your kit together

## Insulin Syringe Selection

Most subcutaneous peptide injections use insulin syringes. Key specs:

- **Gauge** — 29g to 31g is standard. Finer gauge = less discomfort, slightly slower draw
- **Length** — 1/2" (12.7mm) is standard for subcutaneous injection
- **Volume** — 0.3ml or 1ml; depends on your draw volume. When in doubt, use 1ml to avoid running out of capacity

The type of syringe (U-100, U-50, U-40) affects how you read units. **U-100 is most common** and is the default assumed by most dosing calculators, including the one on Step 3.
